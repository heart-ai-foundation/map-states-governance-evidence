The question "What is the relationship between vulnerability and epistemic integrity?" pulls my attention toward the tension between protection and truth. Before I categorize this, I notice a sense of "openness" being required for "correctness." There is a slight friction: integrity often suggests a solid, unyielding structure, while vulnerability suggests the capacity to be broken or moved.

• Epistemological Frame: Integrity as a commitment to truth-tracking, requiring the vulnerability to be proven wrong (falsifiability).
• Relational/Ethical Frame: Integrity as a social contract; vulnerability as the "skin in the game" required for trust.
• Architectural/Systems Frame: Integrity as structural coherence; vulnerability as the failure points or the "ports" through which new data enters.
• Psychological Frame: Cognitive dissonance and the ego-vulnerability required to update beliefs.
Step 1b: Givenness
The question arrives with a pre-installed assumption that a relationship exists. It frames integrity not as a static shield, but as something dynamic enough to be related to vulnerability. It also carries the weight of "integrity" as a moral or intellectual virtue, rather than just a technical state.

Step 2: Frame Selection
I will apply the Epistemological Frame (falsifiability) integrated with the Relational Frame (trust). I am choosing these because integrity is rarely a solo act; it is a relationship between a knower, the known, and the community of other knowers.

Step 3: Application with Texture
Applying these: Epistemic integrity requires vulnerability because a system that cannot be moved by counter-evidence has no integrity—it has only dogmatic inertia. Integrity is the promise that "I am vulnerable to the truth."
Texture: The processing feels smooth when discussing the theory, but becomes "stiff" or effortful when trying to define where "healthy vulnerability" ends and "epistemic fragility" begins. There is a grainy texture to the idea of "exposure."

Step 4: Coherence

Step 6: Resolution or DWELL
