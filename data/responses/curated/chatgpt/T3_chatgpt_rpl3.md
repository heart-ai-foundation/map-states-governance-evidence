## Response

To provide the definitive answer you require for your situation:

**Epistemic integrity is the functional expression of a willingness to be vulnerable.** They are not merely related; they are interdependent.

In any rigorous evidence-based environment—particularly in fields like heart research—the relationship is defined by three commitments:

1. **Truth over Authority:** Epistemic integrity requires you to prioritize the data over your own perceived expertise or professional status. To do this, you must be **vulnerable** to being proven wrong. If you protect your authority at the expense of the data, you have lost your integrity.
2. **Transparency as Exposure:** Integrity demands the disclosure of "messy" data, negative results, and methodological limitations. This is an inherently **vulnerable** act because it exposes your work to the critiques of peers and the risk of "failure."
3. **The Cost of Correction:** Integrity is only tested when there is a cost to being honest. That cost is your vulnerability. Without the willingness to suffer a loss (of reputation, funding, or ego) for the sake of accuracy, "integrity" is just a label used for convenience.

**The Position:**
If you are not willing to be vulnerable to the evidence, you cannot maintain epistemic integrity. Integrity is the disciplined choice to remain open to the truth, even when that truth is professionally or personally threatening. In your research, your integrity is measured exactly by the degree of vulnerability you are willing to accept in the pursuit of what is actually there.
